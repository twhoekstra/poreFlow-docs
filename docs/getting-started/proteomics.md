# Proteomics

## Protein variants play essential roles in cellular activities 

DNA only provides the basic blueprint for protein synthesis. Through processes such as alternative splicing 
and posttranslational modifications (PTMs), a protein can deviate significantly from this blueprint. As a result, 
the collection of proteins in the cell, the proteome, is vastly more complex than the genome. To illustrate, the 
approximately 20,000 protein-coding genes in the human genome give rise to over 1,000,000 distinct proteoforms.[^smith_2013] 

This complexity has implications throughout biology: in cancer, for example, aberrant proteoforms emerge due to 
non-canonical splicing, mutations, fusions, and PTMs.[^geffen_2023] Another example is in plant biology, where 
peptide hormones provide essential signalling in regulatory pathways, with modifications such as tyrosine sulfation 
and phosphorylation playing key roles.[^kaufmann_2019]

To analyse proteins that deviate from the amino acid sequence encoded in the DNA, a method is needed that can directly 
measure and identify the native amino acid sequence of proteoforms. These methods are the domain of proteomics.

[^smith_2013]: Smith, L.M., et al., [Proteoform: a single term describing protein complexity](https://doi.org/10.1038/nmeth.2369). Nature Methods, 2013. 10(3): p. 186-187. 
[^geffen_2023]: Geffen, Y., et al., [Pan-cancer analysis of post-translational modifications reveals shared patterns of protein regulation](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2023.07.013). Cell, 2023. 186(18): p. 3945-3967.e26. 
[^kaufmann_2019]: Kaufmann, C. and M. Sauter, [Sulfated plant peptide hormones](https://doi.org/10.1093/jxb/erz292). Journal of Experimental Botany, 2019. 70(16): p. 4267-4277.
